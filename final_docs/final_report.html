Introduction

This analysis uses the CDC dataset titled “Tobacco Use 3 Months Before and Last 3 Months of Pregnancy.” The dataset provides population-level data on maternal smoking behavior before pregnancy and during the last trimester across participating states in the United States.

The objective of this analysis is to compare smoking prevalence before pregnancy and during the last trimester and to examine regional differences in tobacco use reduction. Because maternal smoking is a well-established risk factor for adverse birth outcomes, reductions in smoking during pregnancy represent an important public health objective.

All analyses were conducted using a fully reproducible workflow.

Interpretation

The results indicate that smoking prevalence consistently decreases during pregnancy across all locations, suggesting that pregnancy may motivate behavioral change. However, the magnitude of reduction varies considerably by location, indicating potential disparities in access to smoking cessation resources or public health interventions.

Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates a consistent reduction in maternal smoking during pregnancy across locations in the United States. However, substantial geographic variation remains, indicating potential disparities in smoking cessation during pregnancy.

These findings highlight the importance of targeted public health interventions to reduce maternal smoking and improve birth outcomes.
